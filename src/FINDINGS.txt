Leibniz Lab (March 2026) — Summary of Findings

This project studies how llm's suggestions and
rule-based reasoning can work together on a knowledge graph.
We begin from a small YAGO3-10 graph (GraphML), then ask an
LLM to generate grounding facts about people, cities,
companies, and countries, and finally run PyReason to infer
new relationships that were not explicitly written in the
starting graph.

In the results image, this process is visible as layered
evidence: known background relations, LLM-provided links,
and stronger inferred links that connect people to countries
and languages through rule chains. In practical terms, the
graph shows that the system can move from isolated facts. 
For example, where someone was born or works.
This leads to meaningful conclusions such as finiding citizenship, residence country, employer
country, and spoken language, in a transparent way.

The findings are that inference is explainable and traceable.
The final links are not random predictions, but consequences
of explicit rules and supporting facts. This is important
for non-technical users because each conclusion in the graph
can be explained in plain language ("this person is linked
to this country because of birthplace and city-country data")
rather than treated as a black-box output.

The visual result supports the core research claim.
Combining graph knowledge with LLM grounding and
symbolic reasoning increases coverage while preserving
interpretability.
